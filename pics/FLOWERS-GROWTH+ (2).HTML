ENGLISH

Jasmine is a beautiful and fragrant flower that is commonly grown in warm climates. Here are some basic steps for growing jasmine:

Choose a sunny spot: Jasmine needs at least 6 hours of direct sunlight each day. Choose a spot that receives plenty of sunlight throughout

the day. Prepare the soil: Jasmine prefers well-draining soil that is rich in organic matter. Mix compost or aged manure into the soil to improve its texture and fertility.

Plant the jasmine: Dig a hole that is twice the size of the root ball of the jasmine plant. Gently remove the plant from its container and place it in the hole. Fill the hole with soil and gently press it down around the plant.

Water the jasmine: Water the jasmine deeply after planting to settle the soil and help the roots establish. Afterward, water the plant deeply once a week or whenever the soil feels dry to the touch.

Fertilize the jasmine: Feed the jasmine with a balanced fertilizer every 4-6 weeks during the growing season (spring and summer).

Prune the jasmine: Prune the jasmine in late winter or early spring to remove any dead or damaged branches and promote new growth.

With proper care and attention, your jasmine plant should thrive and produce beautiful, fragrant flowers

2. MARIGOLD / 2.బంతి పువ్వు

ENGLISH

Marigolds are annual or perennial plants that are easy to grow and care for. Here are some basic steps to follow for growing marigolds:

Choose the right location: Marigolds prefer full sun, so select a location with at least 6 hours of direct sunlight per day. They can tolerate partial shade, but they won't bloom as profusely.

Prepare the soil: Marigolds grow well in well-draining soil with a pH of 6.0 to 7.5. If your soil is heavy and clay-like, add organic matter like compost, peat moss, or well-rotted manure to improve drainage.

Plant the seeds: Marigold seeds can be sown directly into the garden after the last frost or started indoors 4 to 6 weeks before the last frost. Sow the seeds ¼ inch deep and 1 inch apart. Once they sprout, thin them to 6 to 8 inches apart.

Water and fertilize: Keep the soil evenly moist but not waterlogged. Marigolds don't require a lot of fertilizer, but you can add a balanced fertilizer every 4 to 6 weeks.

Deadhead regularly: Pinch off spent blooms to encourage more flowering and prevent the plant from going to seed too soon.

Watch for pests and diseases: Marigolds are generally pest and disease resistant, but keep an eye out for aphids, spider mites, and fungal diseases like powdery mildew.

Harvest and store: You can harvest marigold blooms for use in teas, salves, and other remedies. To store the blooms, dry them in a well-ventilated area away from direct sunlight.

3. ROSE / 3.గులాబీ

ENGLISH

The process of rose growing can be described as follows:

Selection of a suitable location: Roses require plenty of sunlight, good air circulation, and well-draining soil. A location that meets these requirements should be chosen for planting.

Preparation of the soil: The soil should be enriched with organic matter such as compost, manure, or peat moss. The soil should also be well-drained and have a pH level of 6.0 to 6.5.

Planting: Roses should be planted in a hole that is twice as wide and deep as the root ball. The roots should be spread out and covered with soil, being careful not to bury the stem.

Watering: Newly planted roses should be watered deeply to help them establish roots. After that, they should be watered regularly, especially during dry periods.

Fertilization: Roses require regular fertilization to thrive. A balanced fertilizer with an N-P-K ratio of 10-10-10 or 12-12-12 can be used. Fertilizer should be applied in early spring and again in early summer.

Pruning: Pruning is important to encourage new growth, remove dead or diseased wood, and shape the plant. Pruning should be done in late winter or early spring.

Pest and disease control: Roses are susceptible to pests and diseases such as aphids, blackspot, and powdery mildew. Regular inspection and treatment with appropriate pesticides or fungicides can help control these problems.

Winter protection: In colder climates, roses may require winter protection such as mulching or covering with burlap to protect them from frost damage.

By following these steps, a healthy and beautiful rose plant can be grown.

Chrysanthemums are popular flowers that are relatively easy to grow, and their growing mechanism involves a few key factors:

7. ZINNIA / 7.జిన్నియా

ENGLISH

Zinnia flowers are annual plants that are relatively easy to grow. Here are the basic steps for growing zinnias:

Choose a sunny location with well-draining soil: Zinnias require at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight per day and prefer soil that drains well. If your soil is heavy, consider adding organic matter such as compost or peat moss to improve drainage.

Plant seeds or seedlings: Zinnias can be grown from seeds or seedlings. If you're starting from seed, sow them directly in the soil after the last frost date in your area. Cover the seeds with a thin layer of soil and keep them moist until they germinate. If you're starting from seedlings, plant them after the last frost date in your area.

Water regularly: Zinnias need regular watering, especially during hot, dry weather. Water deeply once or twice a week, depending on the weather and soil moisture.

Fertilize: Zinnias benefit from regular fertilization. Use a balanced fertilizer (such as 10-10-10) every 4-6 weeks, following the manufacturer's instructions.

Deadhead regularly: Deadheading (removing spent blooms) encourages zinnias to produce more flowers. Pinch off the dead flowers at the base of the stem.

Watch for pests and diseases: Zinnias are relatively pest and disease-resistant, but keep an eye out for aphids, spider mites, and powdery mildew. If you see signs of a problem, treat it promptly with an appropriate pesticide or fungicide.

By following these steps, you should be able to grow healthy and beautiful zinnias. Good luck!

ENGLISH

Petunias are popular garden flowers known for their bright, colorful blooms and pleasant fragrance. Here's a brief overview of the mechanisms by which petunias grow and develop:

Seed germination: Petunia seeds require warm temperatures (around 70-75°F) and plenty of moisture to germinate. Once the seedlings emerge, they need bright light and a slightly cooler temperature (around 60-65°F) to grow properly.

Root development: Like all plants, petunias rely on their roots to absorb water and nutrients from the soil. As the seedlings grow, their roots become more established, branching out and penetrating deeper into the soil.

Stem growth: Petunias have relatively thin, flexible stems that grow quickly when exposed to plenty of sunlight. As the stems lengthen, they produce small lateral branches that eventually develop into flowering shoots.

Leaf development: Petunia leaves are simple, with a smooth surface and a slightly waxy texture. They are typically green, although some cultivars may have variegated or colored foliage.

Flowering: Petunias are known for their profuse, long-lasting blooms, which come in a wide range of colors and patterns. The flowers are produced at the tips of the stems, and each bloom typically lasts for several days to a week before fading.

Overall, the growth and development of petunias is largely driven by environmental factors such as temperature, light, and moisture, as well as genetic factors that influence traits like flower color and size. By providing the right growing conditions and selecting the best cultivars, gardeners can enjoy the beauty and fragrance of these popular flowers all season long.

This is typically done by taking a cutting from one plant and attaching it to another plant. The two plants will then grow together and form a single plant that combines the characteristics of both.

Division: Hibiscus plants can also be propagated through division. This involves dividing an established plant into smaller sections and planting each section in a new location. 15. COSMO / 15.కాస్మోస్ పుష్పం

Cosmos flowers (Cosmos bipinnatus) are annual plants that are relatively easy to grow and care for. Here is a general overview of their growing mechanism:

Pruning: Cosmos plants do not require pruning, but deadheading spent blooms can encourage more flowers to form and extend the blooming period.

Pests and Diseases: Cosmos plants are generally resistant to pests and diseases. However, occasional issues with aphids, spider mites, or powdery mildew may occur. Treat pests and diseases promptly with the appropriate control measures.

Harvesting: Cosmos flowers can be cut for fresh or dried arrangements. Harvest flowers in the morning before the heat of the day and place them immediately in water. Change the water daily to extend the life of the flowers.

Overall, cosmos flowers are a low-maintenance annual that can provide beautiful blooms throughout the growing season with minimal care.

Aster flowers (Asteraceae family) typically grow from seed, and require certain conditions to successfully germinate and grow. Here are the basic steps involved in growing asters:

Maintenance: As asters grow, they may require staking or pruning to keep them from falling over or becoming too bushy. Deadhead spent blooms to encourage more flowers to grow.

The balsam flower's growing mechanism can be broken down into the following steps:

Pollination: Balsam flowers are pollinated by insects like bees and butterflies, which transfer pollen from the male stamen to the female stigma.

Seed development: After fertilization, the ovary swells and matures into a fruit capsule that contains multiple seeds.

Maturation: As the fruit capsule matures, it dries out and turns brown. The capsule consists of five segments that are joined at the base.

Tension buildup: As the capsule dries, the five segments build up tension, creating a spring-like mechanism.

Dispersal: When the capsule is touched or disturbed, the tension is released, causing the segments to rapidly curl inward and burst open, expelling the seeds in a sudden explosion.

Germination: The seeds are then dispersed over a wider area, increasing the likelihood that they will find suitable conditions for germination and growth.

Bougainvillea is a tropical plant that is known for its beautiful flowers and easy maintenance. Here are the steps for growing Bougainvillea flowers:

Support the Bougainvillea: Bougainvillea can be trained to grow on a trellis or other support structure. Use ties to secure the plant to the support structure as it grows.

Watch for pests and diseases: Bougainvillea is susceptible to pests such as mealybugs, scale insects, and spider mites. Watch for signs of infestation and treat as necessary.

Here are the basic steps for growing tuberose flowers:

Choose a sunny location: Tuberose plants need at least 6 hours of direct sunlight each day, so choose a spot in your garden that receives plenty of sun.

Prepare the soil: Tuberose plants prefer well-draining soil that is rich in organic matter. Amend the soil with compost or aged manure before planting.

Plant the bulbs: Tuberose bulbs should be planted in the spring after the danger of frost has passed. Plant the bulbs 3-4 inches deep and 6-8 inches apart.

Water regularly: Tuberose plants need consistent moisture, so water them deeply once or twice a week, depending on the weather. Avoid overwatering, as this can lead to root rot.

Fertilize monthly: Tuberose plants benefit from regular fertilization. Apply a balanced fertilizer (such as a 10-10-10 formula) once a month during the growing season.

Support the plants: As the tuberose plants grow, they may need support to prevent them from falling over. Use stakes or a trellis to support the plants.

Deadhead spent blooms: As the flowers fade, remove them from the plant. This will encourage the plant to produce more blooms.

Harvest the flowers: Tuberose flowers are typically harvested in the morning when the blooms are fully open. Cut the stems at an angle and place them in water immediately.

By following these steps, you should be able to grow beautiful tuberose flowers in your garden. Thank you!
